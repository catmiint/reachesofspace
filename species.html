Gummisaurs Leviathans
Introduction Anatomy Ecology

Gummisaurs are a species with a partially gummy humanoid form and fully gummy draconic form. Native to space, they are incubated in the heat of dying stars and hatch after a supernova explosion gives them the materials and exposure to properly form. A Gummisaur might never meet their parent(s) or another Gummisaur in their lifetime due to the vastness of the species’ territory despite being a social species. They are rarely lonely, though, as they find friends and homes on planets across the reaches of space.

Loneliness is also staved off by a Gummisaur’s favorite hobby of collecting things! Young Gummisaurs discover what they love and spend the rest of their lives gathering items for their hoards. They can shrink down their hoards and store the items in their gummy for safe-keeping on their travels.

Diet

Gummisaurs primarily feed on stardust, a material that is found generally around stars. This stardust is collected in vials for preservation, and, due to its nature, is also used as currency between gummisaurs frequently! With it, they make a variety of foods, including konpeito and other confections. You may also find gummisaurs eating local cuisine on planets, although this does not generally provide the same level of sustenance and is seemingly more for entertainment value.

Generally, a gummisaur will eat stardust with their mouth, although you may see them absorbing the materials directly into their gummi when that isn’t possible. Exactly why this works and the efficacy are currently being studied. It is assumed, however, that it is not quite as effective as eating through the mouth since they seem to prefer consuming matter that way first.

Magial Capabilities

While magic is not uncommon, not all gummisaurs have access to powers like this. The most common gummisaurs to have magic have perpetual fire, ice, or other elements around them, although this is also not always the case. There isn’t much in regards to limitations for those with magic, although most (not all) end up avoiding magic like mind control for similar reasons as to why they don’t keep animals in their hoards. Commonly, their powers tend to be more specific and specialized - this is mostly due to it being easier to learn one theme or chain of magic and be really good at it than learn multiple types, generally speaking. A lot of gummisaurs end up picking up this skill at a younger age, when the nutrients they pick up is starting to manifest into other traits. The spectrum of magic in the gummisaur species is ultimately quite varied and different for each individual.

Temperament

Most gummisaurs are fairly non-aggressive and act out mostly in self defense or to protect those close to them. While ideologies and concepts of morals and other things in the same vein may vary due to how spread out the species is, as well as their interactions with other species, they aren’t exactly evolved to be incredibly combat-centric. There are gummisaurs who are combat-capable, and even rather proficient in it, but they tend to be more spread out, with the majority of the population being protective at best.

Their strengths typically lie in their ability to communicate and adapt to the cultures around them. Peaceful coexistence with other species is not only something they excel at, but something they frequently have as a goal as well, learning a lot from local communities and keeping this information close to their heart. They tend to have a deep respect for each creature they come across and are more likely to treat them as individuals than objects.
Still, due to the space between each other, there can be some great variance in ideals, meaning that gummisaurs may not always agree with each other and outliers to the generals found in study may be less accurate than the whole of the species.

Hoards

Gummisaurs love collecting all sorts of items, and generally hoard things in a ‘theme’ (although this isn’t always the case). These ‘themes’ can be very specific or broad when used, which can have a lot of unique variations, even amongst gummisaurs with similar hoards.

The main thing they don’t hoard is any sort of living creature. This seems to be most likely related to both morality (most have claimed that ‘it doesn’t feel right’) as well as logistics when it comes to their ability to hoard-store, as atmosphere and preservation are a lot harder to maintain for things that move without making them hold still. This doesn’t stop them from having pets and friends, nor from collecting items related to animals - they just are companions, and not a part of their hoard!
There is a physical limitation as well, as most planets, moons, and other larger objects are simply too large or have too big a gravitational force for a gummisaur to hoard. The main time you might see exceptions to this, however, is when a gummisaur has mixed blood with a Stellion, due to the nature of their chariots.

Physiology Overview

The gummisaur is renowned in the science community for their odd anatomy that gives interesting results every time they’re studied. There is much to be learned about and from these creatures, and, of course, more of this is being learned all the time. Gathered below is a general overview of what information is available in regards to physiology, and may be updated with time as more information is gathered.

Gummisaurs have tails which they use to steer while navigating through space as well as on planets. They also have gummi horns that are used to help protect them and at times act as rudders in addition to their tail(s) for steering while breaking into atmospheres. There are some cases in which gummisaurs lack horns, although those are relatively rare. Despite not all gummisaurs having wings, they can still fly - their gummi has a property that makes it possible to be buoyant in atmospheres in a way comparable to ballast tanks on submarines, where the internal mass is adjusted by the gummisaur for upward and downward movement. This means wings are generally more for altering of direction and increases in momentum instead of being a primary means of flight.

Gummisaurs have an average lifespan of 150-200 Earth Years, although older have definitely been discovered. Studies are still going into learning more about the direct ageing process, as gummisaur cells have some odd properties that aren’t fully understood yet.
Due to how time flows in space, gummisaurs don’t measure age directly, instead gauging maturity off of accomplishments and developing of certain features, such as developing the ability to transform into a humanoid form. The humanoid form requires quite a bit of time gathering ambient elements from space, mental development to utilize them, and physically growing up before it is usable, so it’s a marker of adulthood for gummisaurs.
One may wonder, how do these magnificent creatures breathe on such a large variety of planets and atmospheres, as well as in the depths of space? This is currently not understood and being researched.


Feral

The feral form - or, rather, their natural form - of a gummisaur is made up almost entirely of gummi, giving it a few distinct abilities that their other forms lack. For instance, due to the malleability of gummi, they can often squeeze into spaces smaller than them by adjusting the location of their body mass to allow for a ‘shrinking’ effect in regions temporarily. This form is also fairly robust, able to withstand being tossed around, bitten, or most other measures of force and take minimal damage for the most part. The main downsides to this form is that they are often seen as more animalistic, and being entirely gummi can make them look like a tasty snack or generally be easier to spot due to the generally bright colors they tend to pick up.

In this form, gummisaurs can be found in a large variety of natural sizes, starting as small as around 10cm long (a small to medium sized lizard) to as large as 16km (between the diameters of Mars’ moon Deimos and Phobos). They tend to take on more reptilian shapes, hence their name ending in -saur (lizard), but they can be seen taking other shapes similar to fish, birds, mammals, and even insects at times. Frequently, they can be a mix of these things, allowing for a lot of diversity in this form that can sometimes be echoed in their humanoid form.

This form is also entirely made out of the gummi material, implying that the inside of the humanoid form is potentially also gummi to match, although this theory has yet to be fully researched. This all-gummi construction does give them some extra resistance to damage, increasing their survivability if they do enter a situation where they may take damage.

Humanoid

The main purpose of a humanoid form for a gummi is to help them interact with other species more consistently. It is suspected that this sort of transition is like camouflage, as it may not be inconspicuous to the species they mimic, but it may help them blend in with environments better to avoid predators. This is still being looked into, however, as there is still much to be learned about the creatures that predate on gummisaurs.

This form most resembles whatever species they first interact with, although it can change and adjust with time as well. As such, though, it’s not uncommon to see a gummisaur develop traits after visiting their first planet that end up carrying over into their feral form as well. Even still, it is not always an exact 1-to-1 disguise, as some of their gummisaur traits display on this form such as their horns and tails, as well as some traits being outside of a gummisaur’s reach to fully comprehend enough to replicate. On the other side of things, a gummisaur’s traits can affect their humanoid form as well. For example, a gummisaur with extra limbs may end up taking on more of a centaur-like shape in their humanoid form, even if said form is otherwise relatively human in appearance.
Generally, this form can be seen ranging from 4ft to 8ft high, although outliers are not entirely uncommon due to the variance between gummisaurs and other alien species.

Gender Presentation

Gender is a rather flexible concept for gummisaurs, as varied as the many species they may interact with. This also can reflect in their appearance, as they take on traits related to their gender identity that are in line with the species they have the most exposure to. For example, if a gummisaur identifies as a woman and is frequently around species that have breasts for women, such as mammalian-type species, it is likely that their humanoid form will manifest breasts, but if they are around species without, such as reptilian species, they may not manifest this trait. This applies to most sexual dimorphism, although it may vary as gummisaurs can have a large array of gender identities and physical appearance preferences.

Cardiovascular

One may wonder, how do these magnificent creatures breathe on such a large variety of planets and atmospheres, as well as in the depths of space? The answer is found deep in their chemistry, which allows for most, but not all, of their energy conversion to be through anaerobic glycolysis (or the conversion of sugars without air). Gummisaurs have a surplus of stem cells in their body - primarily in their gummi in their humanoid form - which are able to convert CO2 in their blood back into O2 as needed, as long as they have food. The process produced a surplus of lactic acid, which also boosts their immune system to cancers and illness. Ultimately, this means breathing for a gummisaur is more like taking a break than a required bodily function.

A gummisaur’s blood is made primarily of liquid nitrogen and functions as a coolant to help facilitate changes and promote temperature regulation in general. Their blood color does tend to mimic the species they disguise as in their humanoid form, although it’s not entirely uncommon to see it in an array of colors that don’t quite match.

Gummi

A Gummisaur’s Gummi is a relatively interesting material - it has a highly damage-resistant outer coating, while the inner plasma within tends to take more of a jello-like consistency. Despite the heat that is certain on the inside, the outer coating works well as an insulator, keeping heat in and cold out and making it a relatively safe temperature to the touch. This outer coating seems to also cover all items that are stored in the tail for those with hoard-storing capabilities, preventing the items from all manner of decay or destruction.
Gummi itself is quite malleable - if you put weight on it, it can squish, although never fully flat. Gummisaurs are also quite capable at molding it, even able to take small segments of it to give to others ala ‘Gummi Sharing’, which will be discussed further in the Ecology Section.

As previously mentioned, gummi assists in flight while in a planet’s atmosphere. The mass adjustments have been studied to be related to the internal heat, where superheating the inner plasma lessens the weight in a way that promotes upward momentum, and cooling it encourages descent.

Notably, gummi has the ability to transmit electric currents - it is assumed that this is in relation to the composition of gummi as well as a gummisaur’s blood, although it’s not fully understood and requires some practice on the gummisaur’s part as well. It seems that this capability can allow gummisaurs to use their gummi to interface with cybernetic-type technologies, such as certain ship navigation systems and virtual reality, without the need of the standard cybernetic advancements needed to interact with this technology, such as cyberjacks.

Variant Gummi (Gummi Traits)

Evolution has caused some variants in gummi textures that are assumed to be mostly for defensive and survival related reasons. For example, it is assumed the sour- and sugar-coated variants cause an unpleasant texture and, in the case of the latter, taste in the mouths of predators. It’s thought color-changing is meant to distract predators or blend into surroundings, depending on the gummisaur, and the assumption for liquid-filled is that it helps maintain balance in places with gravity or other strong forces. This isn’t exhaustive, but generally points out some of the ways gummi can vary to a gummisaur’s benefit.

Edibility

It’s well-known that gummi looks a lot like various candies people find mouth-wateringly tasty, and with this brings the question - is gummi edible? It may depend on the anatomy of the person trying to eat it, as some creatures definitely can, but for humans specifically, there are sadly a few issues preventing this. For starters, the human jaw does not provide enough force to break through the protective outer coating. It’s intended to be quite durable, so not much can break through it. Beyond that, the inner contents are hot enough to fully scald the human mouth irreparably.
While there definitely are species who can eat gummi, they are scarce and hard to locate. With further research, the taste of gummi may be determined, however - and potentially even replicable in a way that is edible for other species.

Reproduction

Gummisaurs are bidirectional protogynous hermaphrodites with the potential for self-fertilization. This is to increase species fecundity due to the spread out nature of the species throughout space. Or, in simpler terms, gummisaurs are all born female but can change sex to and from male later in life as they meet potential partners for reproduction. The process of changing sexes takes around ten earth-days.

Gummisaur eggs can be fertilized internally or externally. A pair of gummisaurs can have sex to reproduce with internal fertilization. Or, a currently female-sexed gummisaur can lay a dormant egg. Gummisaur eggs can remain dormant for years thanks to the hard outer coating of their gummi. Currently male-sexed gummisaurs can fertilize dormant eggs, making reproduction possible without sex.

The abilities to lay dormant eggs and to change sexes is what makes self-fertilization possible! A gummisaur could lay an egg as a female-sexed gummisaur, transition to a male sex and fertilize their own egg to have a child on their own.

Gummisaurs are born from eggs that parent gummisaurs lay in stars approaching the end of their life cycle. They are then left there to hatch when the star dies, where they proceed to fend for themselves. The dying star gives them plenty of nutrients to start their life. Once they have grown a bit, or if necessitated to avoid being hunted for one reason or another, they will begin making contact with nearby planets and species.
In some circumstances, if the egg is not laid solidly, the death of the star can apply a lot of outward-expanding force and launch the egg far from the place it was laid. When this happens, the baby gummisaur can end up a little malnourished, although it doesn’t prevent their growth - just stalls it for a good while and leaves the baby extra vulnerable.

Genetics are influenced by parents initially, and then either develop further with age or ‘mutate’ with planetary contact. It is common for a gummisaur to have traits matching parents and trait(s) that don’t appear in either of them that manifested a bit later in their life. This high adaptability to surroundings helps with prevention of predator interaction down the line, as it can make it both harder to track a particular target as well as allow them to evolve specifically to thwart them; it also helps with presentation to other species and adapting to particular planetary ecosystems, and frequently will occur around when a gummisaur visits their first planet. However, sometimes this just happens on its own, without much external influence.

Intraspecies Mingling

Gummisaurs are normally quite social creatures, but the expanse of space can find them spread out quite far, even with their ability to navigate in the stars. As such, how do gummisaurs feel about meeting others of their kind?

Generally, they greet each other with relative goodwill and hospitality, excited to meet one of their own. However, the strength of ties both to their species and also their direct relatives may be somewhat diminished due to a lack of presence, depending on the individual. This may also depend on how close they are to the first species they make contact with, since most gummisaurs supplement their social needs with local denisens.

Gummi Sharing

A way gummisaurs show their care for one another is through the act of sharing their gummi. Most gummisaur are capable of sculpting a small piece of their own gummi to give to another. This offering is then either stored in some sort of carrying device or put into their own gummi and absorbed slowly over time, causing a temporary kaleidoscope of color to be visible.
This practice is most commonly seen during the time of Bloomfaire, as a way to celebrate each other and new growth, although it has been documented rarely outside of this occasion. Sometimes, this has the odd effect of traits similar to the original gummi showing up in the individual who absorbed the offering. This has been most noted during Bloomfaire, but this may be due to the frequency of occurrences during said event.

Interspecies Mingling

Gummisaurs try to maintain a positive relationship with most other species as a whole, wanting intrinsically to be able to blend in as needed. However, some species have ulterior motives towards them, looking to use them for their own purposes.

Poaching

For as wonderful and strange as the gummisaur are as entities, many find a lot of their components useful in various equipment, and so, while illegal in most places, poaching is not unheard of. Due to their coolant-like blood and durable gummi coating, gummisaur parts are highly sought after for space ship engines and interstellar protective gear.

Of course, gummisaurs can offer some of their gummi material, but generally it’s not enough to do large scale constructions and also requires the gummisaur to be willing. This sadly makes them a target of many, and makes their ability to somewhat camouflage with humanoid races all the more important.

Predation

Gummisaurs don’t have a lot of natural predators, due to their hardiness, but there are a few out in the universe! Most predators are so few and far between that the knowledge is scarce, however. Their main predators are found in Leviathans.

Employment

A lot of gummisaurs who are in space frequently have a good sense of navigation, allowing them to be starship navigators and sometimes even directly interact with engines and navigational systems, depending on the ship and the gummis specific abilities. This information does not get stored, but instead helps gummisaur get potential employment if they so choose. Their natural adaptability to most things, including breathing, allows them to be great for recon onto planets with a large variety of atmospheres as well.

Leviathans

Word has slowly been spreading about creatures known as leviathans. Reports are few, with more consistent details fewer, but from what is known, they seem to have an interest in gummisaurs in particular. Despite the lack of information, surveys show that most gummisaurs with damaged gummi had an encounter with a leviathan specifically, and there even is a group dedicated specifically to hunting these foul beasts.

Leviathans appear relatively similar to gummisaurs based on eye-witness reports, with horns and tails that appear to be made of a similar material, if not the same. The main telltale signs of a leviathan involve a floating skull as opposed to a face. They also seem to share both feral and humanoid forms with gummisaurs. There are rumors that this similarity may be a farce to trick gummisaurs into a false sense of security, however, as that seems to be their main, if not only, prey.

Reports also state that leviathans tend to have hard to understand ulterior motives, and are known to place curses on people who are not in alignment with said motives. How much of this is true and not just fearmongering or folklore getting tangled into real-life accounts is anyone’s guess.

Other Species

Crossbreeds

Sometimes, gummisaurs end up mixing with other species, causing some interesting effects. (The following are related to Reaches of Space affiliates- don’t feel restricted by this either!)


Stellions

Gummisaurs are generally a nuisance to Stellions due to their dietary habits. Further than being a nuisance, Gummisaurs could be considered parasitic in nature by Stellions. Crossbreed Gummiaur/Stellions happen when a Stellion attempts to form from what’s left of a star incubating a Gummisaur egg. These young crossbreed Gummisaurs take on equine-like traits and the standard star-tipped ears that Stellions are known for.

Fae Entities from the Flicker Forest

Gummisaur/Fae crossbreeds occur when a Gummisaur egg is laid in a star within Polaris’ or Leviathan’s area of influence. There’s a massive intake of energy into the egg during its hatching. If in Polaris’ or Leviathan’s area of influence, that Gummisaur also absorbs part of their essence and incorporates it into their Gummi. This results in Lanternal and Grimhalla Gummisaurs. These crossbreeds form fully grown thanks to the energies of Polaris and Leviathan, tending more towards the fae end of the spectrum of behavior.

Gummi itself is quite malleable - if you put weight on it, it can squish, although never fully flat. Gummisaurs are also quite capable at molding it, even able to take small segments of it to give to others ala ‘Gummi Sharing’, which will be discussed further in the Ecology Section.

As previously mentioned, gummi assists in flight while in a planet’s atmosphere. The mass adjustments have been studied to be related to the internal heat, where superheating the inner plasma lessens the weight in a way that promotes upward momentum, and cooling it encourages descent. One of the notable discoveries in regards to this and gummisaur anatomy is that their blood is made primarily of liquid nitrogen and functions as a coolant to help facilitate these changes and promote temperature regulation in general. Their blood color does tend to mimic the species they disguise as in their humanoid form, although it’s not entirely uncommon to see it in an array of colors that don’t quite match.

Another notable discovery involves the ability of gummi to transmit electric currents - it is assumed that this is in relation to the composition of gummi as well as a gummisaur’s blood, although it’s not fully understood and requires some practice on the gummisaur’s part as well. It seems that this capability can allow gummisaurs to use their gummi to interface with cybernetic-type technologies, such as certain ship navigation systems and virtual reality, without the need of the standard cybernetic advancements needed to interact with this technology, such as cyberjacks.

Variant Gummi (Gummi Traits)

Evolution has caused some variants in gummi textures that are assumed to be mostly for defensive and survival related reasons. For example, it is assumed the sour- and sugar-coated variants cause an unpleasant texture and, in the case of the latter, taste in the mouths of predators. It’s thought color-changing is meant to distract predators or blend into surroundings, depending on the gummisaur, and the assumption for liquid-filled is that it helps maintain balance in places with gravity or other strong forces. This isn’t exhaustive, but generally points out some of the ways gummi can vary to a gummisaur’s benefit.

Edibility

It’s well-known that gummi looks a lot like various candies people find mouth-wateringly tasty, and with this brings the question - is gummi edible? It may depend on the anatomy of the person trying to eat it, as some creatures definitely can, but for humans specifically, there are sadly a few issues preventing this. For starters, the human jaw does not provide enough force to break through the protective outer coating. It’s intended to be quite durable, so not much can break through it. Beyond that, the inner contents are hot enough to fully scald the human mouth irreparably.
While there definitely are species who can eat gummi, they are scarce and hard to locate. With further research, the taste of gummi may be determined, however - and potentially even replicable in a way that is edible for other species.

Reproduction

Gummisaurs are born from eggs that parent gummisaurs lay in stars approaching the end of their life cycle. They are then left there to hatch when the star dies, where they proceed to fend for themselves. The dying star gives them plenty of nutrients to start their life. Once they have grown a bit, or if necessitated to avoid being hunted for one reason or another, they will begin making contact with nearby planets and species.
In some circumstances, if the egg is not laid solidly, the death of the star can apply a lot of outward-expanding force and launch the egg far from the place it was laid. When this happens, the baby gummisaur can end up a little malnourished, although it doesn’t prevent their growth - just stalls it for a good while and leaves the baby extra vulnerable.

Genetics are influenced by parents initially, and then either develop further with age or ‘mutate’ with planetary contact. It is common for a gummisaur to have traits matching parents and trait(s) that don’t appear in either of them that manifested a bit later in their life. This high adaptability to surroundings helps with prevention of predator interaction down the line, as it can make it both harder to track a particular target as well as allow them to evolve specifically to thwart them; it also helps with presentation to other species and adapting to particular planetary ecosystems, and frequently will occur around when a gummisaur visits their first planet. However, sometimes this just happens on its own, without much external influence.

Intraspecies Mingling

Gummisaurs are normally quite social creatures, but the expanse of space can find them spread out quite far, even with their ability to navigate in the stars. As such, how do gummisaurs feel about meeting others of their kind?

Generally, they greet each other with relative goodwill and hospitality, excited to meet one of their own. However, the strength of ties both to their species and also their direct relatives may be somewhat diminished due to a lack of presence, depending on the individual. This may also depend on how close they are to the first species they make contact with, since most gummisaurs supplement their social needs with local denisens.

Gummi Sharing

A way gummisaurs show their care for one another is through the act of sharing their gummi. Most gummisaur are capable of sculpting a small piece of their own gummi to give to another. This offering is then either stored in some sort of carrying device or put into their own gummi and absorbed slowly over time, causing a temporary kaleidoscope of color to be visible.
This practice is most commonly seen during the time of Bloomfaire, as a way to celebrate each other and new growth, although it has been documented rarely outside of this occasion. Sometimes, this has the odd effect of traits similar to the original gummi showing up in the individual who absorbed the offering. This has been most noted during Bloomfaire, but this may be due to the frequency of occurrences during said event.

Interspecies Mingling

Gummisaurs try to maintain a positive relationship with most other species as a whole, wanting intrinsically to be able to blend in as needed. However, some species have ulterior motives towards them, looking to use them for their own purposes.

Poaching

For as wonderful and strange as the gummisaur are as entities, many find a lot of their components useful in various equipment, and so, while illegal in most places, poaching is not unheard of. Due to their coolant-like blood and durable gummi coating, gummisaur parts are highly sought after for space ship engines and interstellar protective gear.

Of course, gummisaurs can offer some of their gummi material, but generally it’s not enough to do large scale constructions and also requires the gummisaur to be willing. This sadly makes them a target of many, and makes their ability to somewhat camouflage with humanoid races all the more important.

Predation

Gummisaurs don’t have a lot of natural predators, due to their hardiness, but there are a few out in the universe! Most predators are so few and far between that the knowledge is scarce, however. Their main predators are found in Leviathans.

Employment

A lot of gummisaurs who are in space frequently have a good sense of navigation, allowing them to be starship navigators and sometimes even directly interact with engines and navigational systems, depending on the ship and the gummis specific abilities. This information does not get stored, but instead helps gummisaur get potential employment if they so choose. Their natural adaptability to most things, including breathing, allows them to be great for recon onto planets with a large variety of atmospheres as well.

Leviathans

Word has slowly been spreading about creatures known as leviathans. Reports are few, with more consistent details fewer, but from what is known, they seem to have an interest in gummisaurs in particular. Despite the lack of information, surveys show that most gummisaurs with damaged gummi had an encounter with a leviathan specifically, and there even is a group dedicated specifically to hunting these foul beasts.

Leviathans appear relatively similar to gummisaurs based on eye-witness reports, with horns and tails that appear to be made of a similar material, if not the same. The main telltale signs of a leviathan involve a floating skull as opposed to a face. They also seem to share both feral and humanoid forms with gummisaurs. There are rumors that this similarity may be a farce to trick gummisaurs into a false sense of security, however, as that seems to be their main, if not only, prey.

Reports also state that leviathans tend to have hard to understand ulterior motives, and are known to place curses on people who are not in alignment with said motives. How much of this is true and not just fearmongering or folklore getting tangled into real-life accounts is anyone’s guess.
